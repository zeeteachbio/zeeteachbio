1.1.4 Contribution of Muslim Scientists

Muslim scientists played an important role in the early development of biological sciences. They started experiments and observations from the first century of Hijra. Some important contributors are:

1. Jabir Bin Hayan (722–817 A.D)

He was born in Iran and is famous for his work in chemistry. He also wrote books on plants and animals. His well-known books include "Al-Nabatiat" on plants and "Al-Haywan" on animals.

2. Abdul Malik Asmai (741–828 A.D)

He was a great zoologist who wrote many books on animals. His books include "Al-Kheil" (horse), "Al-Ibil" (camels), "Al-Shat" (sheep), "Al-Wahoosh" (wild animals), and "Khalqul Insan", which explains parts of the human body and their functions.

3. Bu Ali Sina (980–1037 A.D)

He is considered one of the greatest Muslim scientists and is known as the founder of medicine. In the West, he is called Avicenna. He identified many diseases such as tuberculosis and meningitis. He also contributed to mathematics, astronomy, physics, paleontology, and music. His famous books are "Al-Qanoon" and "Fil Tib Al-Shafa."
