2. Solving a Biological Problem

2.1.1 explain steps involved in the biological method

2.1.1 explain steps involved in the biological method, i.e., recognition of a biological problem, observation (qualitative and quantitative), building up hypotheses via inductive and deductive reasoning, deducing, devising experiments, inferring and reporting results, proposing theory and putting forward law/ principle (malaria can be taken as an example)

2.1.2 differentiate between hypothesis, theory and law in the context of scientific research

2.1.3 design a solution for a biological problem by following the scientific method:

2.1.4 justify that science is a collaborative field that requires interdisciplinary researchers working together to share knowledge and critique ideas.

Steps in the Biological Method

  1. Recognition of a biological problem

  2. Observation (qualitative and quantitative)

  3. Building hypotheses (inductive and deductive reasoning)

  4. Deduction

  5. Devising experiments

  6. Inferring and reporting results

  7. Proposing theory

  8. Formulating law/principle

1. Recognition of a Biological Problem

A biological problem is identified when scientists notice something unexplained.
For example: Malaria, the problem was that people living near marshy and stagnant-water areas frequently developed recurring fever.
2. Observation (Qualitative and Quantitative)

Obervation is important step in solving biological problem. In this step data is collected by using five senses of vision, hearing, smell, taste and touch.

Observations are of two types:

  • Qualitative observations: Which are based on some quality or characteristics.

    • Symptoms like fever and chills, presence of mosquitoes, environmental conditions.

  • Quantitative observations: The quantivate data is measurable and can be expressed in terms of numbers, which are more accurate.

    • Number of malaria patients, fever cycles, seasonal variations.

3. Building Hypotheses

Hypothesis is a intelligent guess based on the data collected during observations. Hypothesis is a tentative explanation of the observations that might be true.

Charactersistics of good hypothesis

A hypothesis should have following characteristics;

  1. It should be a general statement.

  2. It should be tentative idea.

  3. It should agree with the available observations.

  4. It should be testable and potentially falsifiable.

A hypothesis is developed and tested using reasoning:

  • Inductive reasoning: Moves from specific to general statement.

    • Mosquito abundance where malaria was common, they formed a general idea that mosquitoes might be involved.

  • Deductive reasoning: Deductions are the logical consequences of the hypothesis. To draw deductions hypothesis is taken as true. Deductions involve "if" and "then" logic.

    • If mosquitoes cause malaria, then preventing mosquito bites should reduce cases.

4. Deduction

A logical prediction was made:
If the hypothesis is correct, then controlling mosquitoes should lower the incidence of malaria.

5. Devising Experiments

Experiments are performed to prove if hypothesis is true or not. The deductions drawn from the hypothesis are subjected to rigorous testing. Through experimentation, biologist learns which hypothesis is correct.

Experiments were designed to test the deduction, such as:

  • Using mosquito nets

  • Eliminating stagnant water

6. Inferring and Reporting Results

Drawing a conclusion from the experimental data and sharing the findings.The biologist gathers actual quantitative data from experiments. This data arranged to draw results.

  • When data showed that protected individuals were less likely to get malaria, scientists inferred that the hypothesis was supported.

  • The findings were recorded, documented, and shared with the scientific community.

7. Proposing Theory

A well-supported explanation that is based on many experiments.

  • After repeated verification, a scientific theory was proposed stating that mosquitoes act as vectors transmitting the malaria parasite (Plasmodium).

8. Formulating Law/Principle

A statement that describes a natural fact which always seems to be true.

  • When a theory becomes well established through consistent evidence, it may be expressed as a principle.
    In this case, the established principle is that malaria is transmitted to humans by the bite of an Anopheles mosquito carrying the Plasmodium parasite.
