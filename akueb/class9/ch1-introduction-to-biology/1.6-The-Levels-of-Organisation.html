1. Introduction to Biology

1.6 The Levels of Organisation

1.6.1 describe bioelements as the most basic level of biological organisation

Bioelements
Bioelements are the chemical elements that make up the bodies of living organisms.

They are the simplest and most basic level of biological organization because all structures from molecules to cells, tissues, organs, and organisms are built from these elements.

Explanation:
Living organisms are mainly composed of a few key bioelements. About 99% of the human body is made of carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), nitrogen (N), and small amounts of other elements such as phosphorus (P), sulfur (S), calcium (Ca), sodium (Na), potassium (K), etc.
These elements combine to form biomolecules (like proteins, carbohydrates, lipids and nucleic acids), which then build up cells and higher levels of organization.

Why they are the basic level:

  • They are the starting point for all biological structures.

  • Without bioelements, no molecules or cells can be formed.

  • They determine the chemical properties of life.

1.6.2 explain biomolecules and their types, i.e., micro molecules and macro molecules (based on molecular masses)

Biomolecules

Biomolecules are the chemical compounds formed by bioelements that make up the structure of living organisms and carry out all life processes. They include substances like proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, and nucleic acids, which together build and maintain cells.

Biomolecules are classified into two main types based on their molecular mass:

1. Micromolecules (Small Molecules)

  • Micromolecules are simple, small-sized molecules with low molecular mass.

  • They are usually soluble in water and can easily pass through cell membranes.

  • Examples include water, minerals, amino acids, monosaccharides (simple sugars), and fatty acids.
    They act as building blocks for larger molecules and are essential for cellular reactions.

2. Macromolecules (Large Molecules)

  • Macromolecules are large, complex molecules with very high molecular mass.

  • They are usually formed by joining micromolecules together.

  • These molecules form the structures of cells and perform important functions like genetic information storage, enzyme action, and energy storage.

  • Examples include proteins, polysaccharides (starch, glycogen), lipids, and nucleic acids (DNA, RNA).

1.6.3 differentiate among the levels of organisation of life (subatomic particles, atom, molecules, organelles, cells, tissues, organs, organ systems, organism, population, community, ecosystem, biosphere)

Levels of Organisation of Life

Life is organized in a hierarchical manner, from the smallest particles to the entire biosphere. Each level becomes more complex than the previous one.

1. Subatomic Particles

  • These are the smallest particles inside an atom.

  • Examples: protons, neutrons, electrons.

2. Atom

  • The smallest unit of an element that retains its properties.

  • Examples: Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen.

3. Molecule

  • Formed when two or more atoms chemically combine.

  • Examples: Water (Hâ‚‚O), Glucose, Amino acids.

4. Organelles

  • Tiny specialized structures inside a cell that perform specific functions.

  • Examples: Mitochondria, Ribosomes, Golgi apparatus.

5. Cell

  • The basic structural and functional unit of life.

  • Can function independently.

  • Examples: Neuron, Muscle cell, Bacterial cell.

6. Tissue

  • A group of similar cells performing a specific function.

  • Examples: Muscle tissue, Nervous tissue, Xylem tissue.

7. Organ

  • A structure made of different tissues working together for a specific job.

  • Examples: Heart, Lungs, Leaf.

8. Organ System

  • A group of organs working together to perform major body functions.

  • Examples: Respiratory system, Digestive system, Nervous system.

9. Organism

  • A complete living being, made up of many organ systems (multicellular) or a single cell (unicellular).

  • Examples: Human, Cat, Bacteria, Plants.

10. Population

  • A group of organisms of the same species living in the same area.

  • Example: A school yard of crows, a herd of cows.

11. Community

  • All different populations living and interacting in the same area.

  • Example: Plants + animals + microbes in a pond.

12. Ecosystem

  • A system formed by the community (living components) and the non-living environment (water, soil, sunlight).

  • Example: Forest ecosystem, Desert ecosystem, Pond ecosystem.

13. Biosphere

  • The highest level: all ecosystems of Earth combined.

  • Includes all living organisms and their interactions with air, water, and land.

1.6.5 exemplify that different organ systems work together to form a functioning human body

The human body works as one coordinated unit because different organ systems depend on each other. No system can function alone.

They work together to maintain life, homeostasis, and survival.
