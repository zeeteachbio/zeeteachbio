1. Introduction to Biology

1.7.1 define ‘division of labour’ in living organisms

Definition:
Division of labour in living organisms refers to the specialisation of different cells, tissues, or organs to perform specific functions, so that the whole organism can work efficiently.
(Sindhi: جيوَن واري ورهاستِ جو مطلب آهي ته مختلف جيو گھرڙا (سيل)، ٽشوز يا عضوا پنهنجا پنهنجا خاص ڪم ڪن ٿا.)

1.7.2 Compare division of labour in unicellular and multicellular organisms

Unicellular Organisms

  • Only one cell performs all life functions (nutrition, respiration, excretion, reproduction).

  • No specialisation because the organism is made of a single cell.

  • Example: Amoeba – the same cell moves, eats, digests, and divides.

Multicellular Organisms

  • Made of many cells, each specialised for a specific function.

  • Cells form tissues, organs, and systems, showing clear division of labour.

  • Example: Humans have muscle cells for movement, nerve cells for control, etc.

1.7.3 differentiate between colony-level and tissue-level organisation in volvox and frog respectively

Colony-level Organisation (Volvox)

  • Volvox forms a colony of many similar cells.

  • Cells perform most functions independently, though they live together.

  • There is no true tissue formation.

  • Simple division of labour exists (some cells for movement, some for reproduction).

Tissue-level Organisation (Frog)

  • Frog is a multicellular organism with true tissues (muscle, nerve, epithelial).

  • Tissues combine to form organs and organ systems.

  • Shows advanced division of labour and complex functions.

1.7.4 justify that the volvox colony does not meet the criteria for having the true tissue-level organisation

A Volvox colony cannot be considered to have true tissues because:

  1. Cells are loosely connected
    The cells in the colony are arranged on a gelatinous surface and do not form permanent, tightly bound groups like true tissues.

  2. Cells are mostly identical
    In true tissues, cells show structural and functional specialisation, but Volvox cells are largely similar with only slight variation (e.g., reproductive cells).

  3. Lack of coordinated function
    True tissues work together to perform one combined function.
    In Volvox, each cell performs its own basic functions and coordination is minimal.

  4. No formation of organs
    Tissue-level organisation leads to organs, but Volvox does not form organs or organ systems.
    Since Volvox lacks cell specialisation, strong intercellular connections, coordinated function, and organ formation, it does not meet the criteria of true tissue-level organisation.

1.7.5 evaluate the role of different cell types within a specific tissue/ organ for its overall functioning.

A tissue or organ works properly only when all its specialised cell types perform their specific roles. Each type contributes uniquely, and failure of one type affects the entire organ.

Example: Human Small Intestine (Organ)

  1. Goblet cells
    Produce mucus that protects the lining and helps smooth passage of food.

  2. Absorptive epithelial cells
    Contain microvilli; responsible for absorbing nutrients.

  3. Enterendocrine cells
    Release hormones that regulate digestion.

  4. Smooth muscle cells
    Create peristaltic movement to push food along.
